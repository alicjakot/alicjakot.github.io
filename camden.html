How woman-friendly are streets in London borough of Camden? 

Overview
In this project I investigated how safe and friendly streets of Camden might appear in women's eyes. I analysed freely available data on built enviornment and urban design elemets and classified the borough's streets according to perceived safety. 

Motivation
It is widely known that the urban space shapes the way we feel and behave, however it is often forgotten that men and women experience it differently. Women are more vulnerable to violence and many are still experiencing sexual harassment on streets on a daily basis. These experiences contribute to how women and girls feel in the urban environment. Although London is thought to be one of the safest cities for women in the world (Thomson Reuters Foundation, 2017), the majority of women living there worries about their safety (Greater London Authority, 2018). As London’s vision is “a city of freedoms, opportunities and rights for all” (Greater London Authority, 2018, p. 11) it is important to ensure women and girls feel safe and are not excluded from public space.

Data and methods
Elements of urban design impact women’s perception of safety and show exclusion of women in public space. For the purpose of the analysis, the following variables were included: 

1. street lights - after dark, pedestrians depend on artificial street lighting. It contributes greatly to women’s perception of safety, as it increases visual accessibility (Johansson et al., 2020) and reduces the feeling of being vulnerable to crime in cities 
2. trees – previous research have demonstrated that the feeling of safety is highly correlated with urban greenery (Johansson et al., 2020; Mouratidis, 2019) 
3. pavement width – narrow pavements exclude people with buggies
4. access to public toilet – as toilets are “basic infrastructure that ensures dignity”, the lack of public toilets is the sign of exclusion of women in public spaces (Cosgrave et al., 2020, p.5) 

Prior to analysis data was cleaned, transformed and normalised. To classify Camden's streets I used k-means clustering method.
